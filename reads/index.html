Reads/

I read quite a few Science Communication books throughout my bachelor times in Natal. Undoubtedly, titles such as The Drunkard's Walk and Lonely Planets greatly contributed to my initial scientic education. Although I still very much harbour a desire to read on various scientific subjects (I really like Physics, for instance), at some point one must focus on a specific field considering the short half-life of a human begin, and thus my reading of this kind of genre is no longer as diverse as it once was. Moreoever, by the end of my PhD in Copenhagen, I became rather interested in Literature, and I have been trying to catch up reading the Classics. Should you be curious to see which books I have read on these fronts, you are welcome to check my profile on goodreads.

Having said that, what I present below is a list of titles revolving round the two subjects I intend to expertise, namely Evolutionary Biology & Philosophy of Science. The books I have already read (at least once) are marked in green, and a short commentary is provided. Please acknowledge that this is for my own personal recording, and my impressions on these titles should be taken with care. Else, I have decided to list them in a chronological order because I find it revealing to realise how things unfolded historically. For instance, Huxley published his book on the evolution of Homo sapiens 8 years before Darwin presented his own work on this subject; or that Wallaced published his book on Natural Selection 20 years after Darwin's famous title had been published.

Evolution | Carl Zimmer (2001)

This is the thrid book I read within this theme — a title that intends to explain Evolution to the general public. Although well-meant, I found that this title is not the best choice available. I think that the 2009 book Why Evolution is True by Jerry A. Coyne (see below) is a better work. Zimmer´s book resembles the also 2001 title What Evolution Is by Ernst Mayr (see below) — they are formed by a collection of facts on Evolution but they still fail to explain our current understanding of the natural laws governing biological change on Earth. Besides, this book contains at least a few factural errors that are quite disturbing to accept. However, I must say that a newer version is available and I am not sure if these errors were corrected. Despite all this, this title is worth reading without a doubt!

Improbable Destinies | Jonathan B. Losos (2017)

This book was repeatedly (pun unintended) recommended to me by my colleague and friend Waldir Miron. After a couple of months having to explain Waldir over and over again why I had not read it yet, I was finally glad when I was able to tell him that now I have. Written by Professor Jonathan B. Losos, who is the William H. Danforth Distinguished Professor at the Washington University in St. Louis, this book is a great introduction to the highly debated subject of convergent evolution. Truth be told, this was the subject that interested me the most when I first started my studies in Biology. Unfortunately, I never really had the chance to work on it, but I still have the intention to so do.

The Evolution of Beauty | Richard O. Prum (2017)

This was another great recommendation by my colleague and friend Waldir Miron. I really appreciated reading this title by Richard O. Prum, who is the Head Curator of Vertebrate Zoology in the Yale Peabody Museum of Natural History — Yale University. Prof. Prum has a long and vast experience working on Avian Biology, and here he brings to light an important issue of Modern Evolutionary Biology — the fact that Darwin's original ideas on Sexual Selection or forgotten or even deliberately shunned. The author elouquently makes the point that we ought to reincorporate a Darwinian view on the subject before we can have any hope to truly compreend the diversity of Life on the planet.

The Rise and Fall of the Dinosaurs | Steve Brusatte (2019)

This book was my companion during the first lockdown caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. Maybe it was not the best idea to be reading about an almost extinct lineage of animals during such dreadful times, but it just happened to be. Written by Steve Brusatte, who is the Professor of Palaeontology and Evolution of the University of Edinburgh, this title is probably the best description of what has transpired during the evolution of these majestic beings. Thus, I am looking forward to reading Steve's new book this time on the evolution of mammals. Steve is also very active on Twitter, so you might want to follow him should you want to have a lively source of paleontological news.
